A Redesign of The Imagination Library of Grant County’s Platfrom

Our main goal for this project was to identify and solve issues surrounding information architecture and hierarchy, navigation, and usability. With those goals in mind we reached out to the Imagination Library of Grant County, with whom one of our team members had a connection with, and asked them if we could take a stab at updating their platform, to which we were warmly given an okay! Needless to say, we were thrilled. Here’s how it went!

    From the Ground Up

    To start we went ahead and visited the site to see what it was that we were working with. We were greeted with the above, to which we all immediately noticed some glaring issues, however despite the apparent hurdles, we also all found details which we thought were endearing as well. After going over the entire site a few times individually, we came together to review it, and brainstorm some of the loudest issues we all noticed.

    “Outdated, and not very Dolly!”

    After we detailed what we all thought were the most pressing items that needed resolving, we decided the best way to get the full scope of what needed to be worked on was to set up a test where users viewed the landing page for five seconds, giving their initial impressions afterwards. We had a turnout of 13 results, which cumulatively validated our initial concerns. We recorded some of the more notable impressions that we felt gave us the most direction.

    Visiting the Site

    What else can be changed?

    Following our user testing, we further felt out what was on our plate by creating a site map, so we could gauage the state of the site’s navigation against a usability hueristic. Some of the notes we took during and following our mapping:

    • Click-through rate was overall high

    • Amount of content each page contained was very minimal

    • Certain page names seemed redundant

    • Content of certain pages didn’t necessarily correlate to indicated page titles

    Key Issues to Tackle:

    As we concluded our main phase of researching, we felt that we had a lot of work ahead of us. We took a moment to summarize what we needed to prioritize for ourselves:

    • Complete visuals update to bring site in line with modern trends and usability standards, while also staying on theme

    • Full reconstruction of information architecture and UX

    • Cutting unnecessary or outdated information

    With these goals set in mind we began working on the foundation for the direction that our redesign was going to head in. Our main concern was making sure that our work was efficient, so we started with our logistical concerns first, so that our visual philosophies wouldn’t clash with the practical application of our redesign.

    Ideation

    Affinity for What?

    To begin we set up question based interviews aimed at attaining a sense of what general attitudes towards nonprofits were. We then organized an affinity diagram, so we could organize the ideas that we came away with and thought were relevant perspectives towards appealing to user desires, needs and preferences. We laid out our notes and associated them with a general list of topics.

    Revisiting the Site

    Our second step was to restructure the site’s information hierarchy entirely. With the overly convoluted site map we laid out previously, we felt that quicker access to key points of interest was a mainstay logistical point that needed to be covered before any further movement. We took the previous 34 pages, and reduced them down to 10. This gave us a clearer idea of our ore practical tasks:

    • Generate creative and effective ways to consolidate information without overwhelming users with text

    • Discern what sort of information could be moved where, and what could be removed completely

    Putting Pen to Anchor Point

    Following our site remapping, we thought about item number one: the best way to visually organize everything. As we brainstormed, we noticed that one standout issue that our research yielded was that the site was overly text heavy. That, in addition to already having to reconsolidate existing information, seemed like a significant hurdle for us. We decided that the best was to present users with the information they wanted was to allow them to access what they wanted, at their speed using carousels/cards. This idea helped avoid scrolling fatigue, and required minimal searching through the page for pertinent information, so we ran with it, and developed some lofi wireframes to go forward with.

    Lo-Fi v1.0

    Lo-Fi v2.0

    Next we began ideating on what we thought the best aesthetic direction was. Since the site serves as a platform for a nonprofit centered around children, we thought about how we could best bring out the childhood excitement of reading in our design. We knew we wanted to signal the child centered nature of the nonprofit, while also making the visuals refined enough for adults to enjoy as well. We collaged together a mood board with some of the following visuals serving as our basis, focusing on colors as a primary point of interest.

    Based on our inspiration from our moodboard, we devised a style guide that was inline with the colors and feelings we wanted users to experience. We decided on a pallet that bordered on pastelle-esque, but aired towards a saturation that was just bright enough to still be able to pop where we wanted it to.

    Testing and Iteration

    Getting it Right

    As we finalized some of our basic stylistic choices, we began working on some mid-fidelity iterations that took some pages from our original lo-fi mockups. With grant county New Mexico in mind, one of our main iterations was heavily centric around a cutout mesa background. Another of our heading iterations was more focused around iconography more centric to the imagination Library itself, rather than the identity of the branch.

    While both of our main contenders had features we were all drawn to, neither one accomplished everything we’d wanted. We took the best aspects of both and combined them in a way that we felt was both visually and technically accomplishing what we’d been aiming for. Our end result accomplishes our goals:

    Restructure information hierarchy using a card system to present information in a way that is quickly recognizable and accessible while ensuring the end product feels and looks modern while also staying true to original sentiments and messages.

    Next Steps and Reflections

    As we concluded our final product we felt we succeeded in many areas, while also having many places where we felt we had room to improve. Our process during our mid-fi iteration phase could have been more organized and efficient, as we could have spent less time getting caught on small details and pre-planning.

    We also Had a few items that escaped us in our development process that we thought were important enough to include on our to-do list provided we revisit this project:

    • Further develop out our mobile stylings for the platform

    • Focus more on micro interactions, making the site feel modern in line with it’s updated visuals

    • Build out an updated ‘donations’ page that integrates with the payment platform currently in use

    • Build out a page where users can access a list of books slated to be sent to children in future months

    We’ve been in contact with our connection at the Grant County branch, and have plans to hand off the designs when they’re in a position to invest time into restructuring.
